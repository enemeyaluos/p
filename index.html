About the exhibition

The exhibition
Flops ?!

During our visit to the Musée des Arts et Métiers, we discovered various objects that had failed. This exhibition allowed us to look at failure differently — no longer as an end, but as a necessary step in the invention process.

The exhibition questions the engineer, the designer, the salesperson, the advertiser and even the user to dissect the mechanisms of failure and better understand what went wrong.

"Drawing on several case studies, the Flops ?! exhibition explores the causes of failure across different technical sectors (transport, telecommunications, mechanics…) or sometimes less technical ones (games and toys, graphic communication, psychological bias…)."

"And because some flops ultimately turn out to be tops, the exhibition explores unexpected success stories: a good idea that was ahead of its time, an immature technology, or an object whose use was repurposed."

— Musée des Arts et Métiers

The objects that stood out

🚢 Transport
Naval architecture · 1628
The Vasa Model

Built to be the most impressive warship of its era, the Vasa sank less than an hour after setting sail due to a stability issue. A design flaw that perfectly illustrates how ambition without method leads to failure.

📍 Stockholm · Sweden
🚀 Space
NASA · 1999
Mars Climate Orbiter

A $327 million space mission ruined by a simple unit conversion error. One team used miles, the other kilometres. The probe burned up in the Martian atmosphere. Miles vs kilometres: 1 – 0.

🌍 NASA · Mars Mission
🎲 Games
Games & communication · 1989
"Trump: The Game"

Launched in 1989, this board game bearing Donald Trump's image was meant to capitalise on his fame. Despite a large marketing budget, sales were catastrophic. Too complex, too personality-focused: a memorable commercial flop.

🇺🇸 Milton Bradley · United States
What we take away

Failure,
the engine of innovation

What the "Flops ?!" exhibition taught us is that behind every great success lies a series of failures. The Vasa, the Mars Climate Orbiter, or even the Minitel: objects that failed their original purpose, but taught us something essential.

The exhibition challenges our relationship with failure and invites us to see it no longer as a shame, but as a step in the creative process — a vision particularly relevant in our computer science studies.
